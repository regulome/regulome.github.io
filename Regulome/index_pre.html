Following Francis Collins emphasis on intramural-extramural collaborations and the application of cutting-edge tools to research, the NIH Mouse Regulome Project was established in 2011. The primary objective of this program is to elucidate how gene expression is regulated in the mouse genome by combining the latest technology developed at intramural and extramural NIH labs. At each stage of the project different extramural investigators are invited to participate.



Stage I: 2011-2013

Participating Laboratories:

Rafael Casellas (NIAMS-NCI), David Levens (NCI), Keji Zhao (NHLBI), Lino Tesarrollo (NCI), Teresa Przyticka (NLM), Jan Hoeijmakers (Erasmus Medical Center), and Wim Vermeulen (Erasmus Medical Center, the Netherlands).

Objectives and main findings:

Genomic tools were applied to explore how mRNA output is controlled during ontogeny. Using primary ES cells and B lymphocytes the studies revealed that transcriptomes are amplified as G0 cells enter the cell cycle. Two factors were identified as key drivers of transcriptome amplification: Myc and the TFIIH complex. In both cases the factors are maintained at basal levels in G0 but their expression is rapidly enhanced as cells are stimulated. Conversely, PolII is recruited at similar levels to G0 and cycling cell promoters.

References:

Kouzine et al. Global Regulation of Promoter Melting in B lymphocytes. Cell, 2013.


Stage II: 2013-2014

Participating Laboratories:

Rafael Casellas (NIAMS-NCI), Yijun Ruan (Jackson labs), Gordon Hager (NCI), Keith Joung (Harvard), Michel Nussenzweig (Rockefeller), and Qiang Pan-Hammarstrom (Karolinska Institute).

Objectives and main findings:

Nuclear interconnectivity between gene regulatory elements was explored by means of genome editing, Digital Genomic Footprinting, and ChIA-PET technologies. A key finding was the observation that the enhancer landscape changes drastically during mammalian development. Even for broadly expressed genes (e.g. Pim1, Myc), promoters often associate with a completely different set of enhancers in progenitors (ES) and fully differentiated cells (B lymphocytes). A side finding was that highly active super-enhancers in B cells are highly susceptible to DNA damage, a feature that helps explain why B cells are particularly prone to the formation of chromosomal translocations and tumor development during the immune response.

References:

Kieffer-Kwon et al. Interactome maps of mouse gene regulatory domains reveal basic principles of transcriptional regulation. Cell, 2013.
Qian et al. B Cell Super-Enhancers and Regulatory Clusters Recruit Tumorigenic Activity. Cell, 2014

Available Data:

Raw datasets the ChIP-seq data in activated B cells (other than CTCF) and CH12 cells, DHS-seq in activated B cells, RNA-seq, whole genome methylation (other than ES cells), and ChIA-PET reported in this paper can be accessed through the Short Read Archive with the accession number SRP029721.

Tracks for ChIA-PET, DHS hotspots, DHS footprints, and CpG methylation for activated B cells and ES cells are available here.(Due to the large file size, you may have to reload the download multiple times).

Stage III: 2014-present

Participating Laboratories:

Rafael Casellas (NIAMS-NCI), Erez Lieberman Aiden (Baylor), Yijun Ruan (Jackson labs), Gordon Hager (NCI), Lino Tessarrollo (NCI), Monique Floer (Michigan), Melike Lakadamyali (Barcelona), Hari Schroff (NIBIB), Zhe Liu (Janelia Farm, HHMI), Fred Alt (Harvard), and Francisco Asturias (Scripps).

Objectives and main findings:

The architecture of chromatin in B and ES cells is being explored by a combination of in situ HiC, ChIA-PET, genome editing, nanoscopy, single molecule microscopy, and EM technologies. Based on these approaches three manuscripts are currently being prepared which define the roles of histone acetylation and the Mediator complex in chromatin decondensation and nuclear topology. The ultimate goal is to assess at a single cell level how these processes control transcription in the mouse genome.
